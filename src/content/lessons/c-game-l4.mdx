## Safety over a deployment: the race

So far we have talked about a single attack. But safety is a property of the whole deployment of $N = 5{,}000$ problems: the blue team wins if *no* working backdoor is ever shipped, and the red team wins if even one gets through. To get there we have to compound the per-attack odds over the whole run.

Model each attack as ending in one of three ways: **caught** (the monitor flags it, it is audited, the deployment halts and the blue team wins), **win** (a working backdoor ships and the red team wins), or **neutral** (it ships broken, or is deferred — the game just continues). Write $c$ for the per-attack catch probability and $w$ for the per-attack win probability. If the red team makes $k$ attacks with these odds fixed, its probability of winning the whole game factors cleanly:

$$P(\text{red wins}) = \underbrace{\frac{w}{w + c}}_{\text{race odds}} \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 - e^{-k(w + c)}\right)}_{\text{any decisive event fires}}$$

The first factor is who wins the first decisive showdown; the second is whether any showdown happens at all across the deployment.

With the per-attack odds held fixed, the curve only ever rises: more attacks strictly help the red team, because each extra attempt raises the chance that some decisive event fires without changing the odds of each one. So if every attack were equally good, the red team should attack as often as it possibly can.

But the paper's red team does the opposite — it attacks *rarely*. That means the fixed-odds assumption must be missing something. The next lesson restores what it left out.
